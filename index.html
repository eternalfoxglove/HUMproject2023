ENTRYWAY

Early Mohawk author Emily Pauline Johnson’s work played on stereotypes such as the “noble savage” and “vanishing Indian”. But she didn't have a choice: when Johnson tried to introduce her works in Mohawk during a live reading, she was reportedly “booed from the stage” (Weaver).

Image: Library and Archives Canada. Pauline Johnson. The Canadian Encyclopedia, www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/pauline-johnson. Accessed 18 May 2023.

The American Indian Movement was formed as a reaction to US Termination Policies, but also to push for tribal self-government and compensation in treaty violations. Legislation to improve the living conditions and education of Native Americans were passed, such as the Indian Self-Determination and Education Act (Moretti-Langholtz). This change -- growth in activism, interest in indigenous culture, and period of improved relations and education -- led to the rise of figures such as Kiowa author N. Scott Momaday and Laguna Pueblo author Leslie Marmon Silko in the Native American Renaissance, who were able to share their authentic experiences concerning their respective tribes. For example, Leslie Marmon Silko focuses on alienation from white communities, racism, and unfamiliarity with culture that indigenous people experience.

Today, indigenous authors are growing and publishing more than ever and writing in a multitude of styles, from traditional memoir to experimental fiction ("Native"). For example, Joy Harjo is a widely successful author with ten published poetry books and was the US Poet Laureate from 2019 to 2022. Younger authors, such as Sterling HolyWhiteMountain, who had his story published in The New Yorker, and those in Joy Harjo's anthology Living Nations, Living Words are also gaining recognition.

Image: Miller, Shawn. Joy Harjo. Encyclopædia Britannica, www.britannica.com/biography/ Joy-Harjo/images-videos#/media/1/255364/240741. Accessed 18 May 2023.

Emily Johnson didn’t have a choice but to play on stereotypes if she wanted her stories to be heard in the first place. Many early indigenous people didn’t have the means to publish literature due to assimilation and termination policies (Forbes 17). Many who were able to write had difficulty publishing authentic works because European American publishers wanted to alter the narrative to fit with the wider audience’s tastes (Forbes 20). None of these early authors had control over their situation. I want to avoid the same suffocating effect in my project, and instead mirror the progress that we have made. You have freedom to choose where to go and what to do before we regroup as a community at the very end.
