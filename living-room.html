LIVING ROOM

“In communities that have too often been fractured and rendered dysfunctional by the effects of more than five hundred years of colonialism, to promote community values means to participate in the healing of the grief and sense of exile felt by Native communities…”

Jace Weaver

The living room is a place of gathering, a place where families can see each other again after a long day. Although it might not always be a place of relaxation, it ideally creates a sense of belonging and connection between its inhabitants. Similarly, some Native American texts are used to create connections between diasporic or migrating tribe members, or even across multiple different indigenous groups. Doing so, Weaver says, helps indigenous people heal from the traumatizing effects of imperialism and colonialism.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Dr. Craig Santos Perez is a Pacific Islander from Guam with indigenous CHamoru origins. He is an English Professor at the University of Hawai'i, and has published numerous books of poetry, monographs, critical essays ("About").

KEY TAKEAWAY

Writing about community is important not just for Native Pacific Islanders, who are extremely dispersed according to Perez, but for indigenous people in general. Advocating for change requires all to be united. For example, indigenous people were unable to resist European colonization and Westward Expansion because they had extremely different cultures, languages, and alliances that made unification impossible. While recognizing the uniqueness of each tribe and even individual is vital, utilizing community values in literature -- such as how Perez plays on a shared experience of many Pacific Islanders -- can unite distinct tribes and even ethnicities to promote change in the present day.

I chose to include Perez’s poem in this project because those on the mainland often forget there are Native Americans far in the Pacific. The small islands, such as the Marianas Islands mentioned in Perez’s poems, are part of the US but almost impossible to remember. Personally, I had forgotten that Hawaii natives are still considered Native Americans until the annexation of Hawaii was brought up in US history. It made me wonder what it was like to live on such a faraway island, which encouraged me to seek a Pacific Islander’s story.
