Louise Erdrich defines “community” as "a place that has been ‘inhabited for generations’, where ‘the landscape becomes enlivened by a sense of group and family history’"

qtd. in Weaver

The study is a place of writing and learning. In this case, it is used as a place to record and remember history. Ojibwe author Louise Erdrich describes the community as a landscape filled with history; thus, Native American authors may write about the landscape as a way to preserve family, tribal, and community history long after the last person who truly remembers the culture has disappeared. This way, later generations can learn about the culture again. Furthermore, writing about detrimental changes in landscape or community allows indigenous authors to discuss losses in culture and identity.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Kiowa author N. Scott Momaday is one of the first indigenous authors associated with the Native American Renaissance after his first novel, House Made of Dawn, won the Pulitzer Prize. His parents, both teachers, taught on reservations in the Southwest and fostered Momaday's love for literature ("N. Scott Momaday").

I would like to clarify that not all Native Americans today have extremely close ties with nature, or value traditions that greatly involve nature. As Choctaw and Cherokee Mr. Dalton Savage states, “A lot of native people in Oklahoma don’t really associate with their tribes; they don’t really participate in [traditional] events”. However, some communities rely on the environment, which modern events are causing damage to. Much of it involves health issues, and not necessarily “vanishing” or “dying community” issues.

Some Alaskan natives are facing flooding, evacuations, and challenges to traditional subsistence activities due to climate change (Depenbrock).
